\section{Introduktion}\label{stycke1}

When modelling bear populations, age determination constitutes a key component for understanding and predicting population dynamics. More recently, DNA-based methods have been developed (Nakamura et al., 2025), which may provide age estimates with better error characteristics for population level inference.

Both the magnitude and the distribution of age determination errors may differ between methods as well as depend on the true age of the individual. For example, very young individuals cannot have their age underestimated below zero, whereas older individuals may exhibit larger errors. Additionally, accurately estimating the age of individuals near the threshold between non-reproductive and reproductive age is particularly important for population-level analyses.

It is thus important to assess how different forms of age determination error affect estimated population age distributions, and to what extent conclusions drawn from observed age data may be sensitive to these errors.

To investigate how age determination error affects estimated population age distributions, a simulation-based approach will be used. First, a baseline age distribution representing the true population age structure will be constructed using a simple population matrix model, inspired by models presented in Caswell (2001) and parameterized with data from Střen m.fl. (2021).

Next, age determination error will be modelled as a random variable whose distribution may depend on the true age of the individual. To this purpose we will use n different distribution. A baseline case where there error is 

Several plausible error distributions will be considered, with their parameters chosen so that the standard deviation of the distribution matches roughly the RMSE of the observed residuals for each age determination method. 

As a final step, a deconvolution matrix will be constructed to examine whether it is possible to recover the true age distribution from observed age data, given knowledge of the age determination error distribution. This matrix can then be used to assess the accuracy of population-level inference for different age determination methods.


\section{Theory}\label{stycke2}
\subsection{Matrix population modells}\label{pop_model_matrix}

To obtain a suitable idea of the population age distribution over time we use a population matrix model \cite{caswell2001} which we will describe shortly in this section. The age class $i=1,2,... m$ is the discretization of the age $x$ to age-class $i$ with  $i - 1 \leq x \leq i$. The number of individuals for each age class $i$ is then given by $n_1,n_2,...,n_m$.


Following \cite[Eq.~2.1]{caswell2001} the number of $n_1$ individuals at time $t+1$ can be projected with  
$$n_1(t+1) = f_1n_1(t) + f_2n_2(t)+... + f_in_i(t)$$

Here $f_i$ denotes the reproductive rate per-capita. That is the average number of offspring an individual of age class $i$ has. For $i > 1$ the number of individuals in an age class $n_i$ at time $t+1$ is simply given by their survival probaility $(s_{i-1})(n_{i-1})$ at the previous timestep $t$. Together we can write the equation in matrix form for all $n_i$ at time $t+1$. 

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
	n_1 \\
	n_2 \\
	\vdots \\
	\vdots \\
	n_m
\end{pmatrix}  (t+1) =
\begin{pmatrix}
	f_1   & f_2   & f_3   & \cdots & f_m \\
	s_1   & 0     & 0     & \cdots & 0   \\
	0     & s_2   & 0     & \cdots & 0   \\
	\vdots& \vdots& \ddots& \ddots & \vdots \\
	0     & 0     & \cdots& s_{m-1}& 0
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
	n_1 \\
	n_2 \\
	\vdots \\
	\vdots \\
	n_m
\end{pmatrix}  (t)
\]

\subsection{Computing age distribution from a Leslie matrix}\label{sec:age_distribution}

The stable age distribution of a population can be obtained from the Leslie matrix $\mathbf{A}$ by computing its dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ and the corresponding right eigenvector $\mathbf{w}$. 
This eigenvector gives the relative proportions of individuals in each age class at equilibrium, and can be normalized so that the entries sum to 1.

The existence of a real, non-negative eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ that is greater than or equal in magnitude to all other eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ of a non-negative, primitive matrix $\mathbf{A}$ is guaranteed by the Perron-Frobenius theorem \cite{caswell2001}. 

If $\mathbf{A}$ is primitive, then as $t \rightarrow \infty$, the population vector $\mathbf{n}_t$ satisfies
\[
\frac{\mathbf{n}_t}{\sum_i n_{i,t}} \to \mathbf{w},
\]
where $\mathbf{w}$ is the normalized right eigenvector corresponding to $\lambda_1$. 
This represents the **stable age distribution** of the population.


\subsection{Age determination errors}\label{age_errors}

Once the stable age distribution of the population has been obtained, we simulate the effect of age determination errors. 

Let $\epsilon$ denote the measurement error in age, which may depend on the true age $X$:

\[
\epsilon \mid X \sim N(0, f(X)^2).
\]

The observed age $Y$ is then given by

\[
Y = X + \epsilon.
\]

Applying this error to the theoretical distribution produces the observed age distribution. 
This can be represented using a misclassification matrix $M$, where $M_{ij}$ denotes the probability that an individual of true age class $i$ is observed in age class $j$.

If $\mathbf{p}_x$ is the vector of true age proportions and $\mathbf{p}_y$ the observed proportions, then

\[
\mathbf{p}_y = M \mathbf{p}_x .
\]


\subsection{misclassification matrices} \label{subsec:missclasification}

For discrete age classes, define a misclassification matrix $M$ such that

\[
M_{ij} = P(Y = j \mid X = i)
\]

is the probability that an individual of true age class $i$ is observed in age class $j$.  
Let $\mathbf{p}_x$ be the vector of true age proportions (from the stable distribution $\mathbf{w}$), and $\mathbf{p}_y$ the observed age proportions. Then the observed distribution is

\[
\mathbf{p}_y = M \mathbf{p}_x.
\]

\subsection{Stable age distribution and age measurement errors}\label{age_stable_errors}

Let $\mathbf{A}$ be a Leslie matrix describing the age-structured population dynamics. 
The population vector at time $t$, $\mathbf{n}_t$, evolves as

\[
\mathbf{n}_{t+1} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{n}_t.
\]

\paragraph{Stable age distribution.}  
The dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ of $\mathbf{A}$ and its corresponding right eigenvector $\mathbf{w}$ satisfy

\[
\mathbf{A}\mathbf{w} = \lambda_1 \mathbf{w}, \quad \mathbf{w} \ge 0.
\]

By the Perron--Frobenius theorem \cite{caswell2001}, $\lambda_1$ is real and non-negative, and as $t \to \infty$, the normalized population vector converges to the **stable age distribution**:

\[
\frac{\mathbf{n}_t}{\sum_i n_{i,t}} \longrightarrow \mathbf{w}, \quad \text{with } \sum_i w_i = 1.
\]

\paragraph{Age measurement errors.}  
Let $X$ denote the true age of an individual, and suppose age determination is subject to measurement error $\epsilon$:

\[
Y = X + \epsilon, \quad \epsilon \mid X \sim N(0, f(X)^2),
\]

where $f(X)$ specifies the standard deviation of the error as a function of true age.



\paragraph{Summary.}  
This formulation allows us to combine the theoretical population structure (from the Leslie matrix) with realistic measurement error in age, producing an observed age distribution that accounts for both **population dynamics** and **age-determination uncertainty**.

\subsection{Expectation-Maximization algorithm (EM-algorithm)} \label{sec:em_alg}


The EM algorithm is a general-purpose method for computing maximum likelihood estimates in the presence of incomplete or latent data (\cite{mclachlan2008}). Let $\mathbf{Y}$ denote a discrete random vector corresponding to the observed (incomplete) data, and $\mathbf{X}$ a discrete random vector representing the complete data. If $\mathbf{X}$ were fully observable, we could directly compute the complete-data log-likelihood function $\ell_c(\theta)$ based on a realization $x$.

However, in practice we only observe $y \in \mathcal{Y}$, the sample space of $\mathbf{Y}$. The complete-data sample space $\mathcal{X}$ is related to $\mathcal{Y}$ through a many-to-one mapping, i.e., a surjection from $\mathcal{X}$ to $\mathcal{Y}$, where multiple elements $x \in \mathcal{X}$ correspond to the same observed value $y$.

The probability mass function of $\mathbf{Y}$ is therefore given by
\begin{equation}
	p_Y(y;\theta) = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}(y)} p_X(x;\theta),
	\label{eq:pmfY}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{X}(y) = \{x \in \mathcal{X} : h(x) = y\}$.

Since the complete data $\mathbf{X}$ is not observable, the complete-data log-likelihood $\ell_c(\theta)$ cannot be evaluated directly. Instead, the EM algorithm considers its conditional expectation given the observed data $y$ and a current parameter estimate $\theta^{(t)}$:

\begin{equation}
	Q(\theta \mid \theta^{(t)}) = \mathbb{E}_{\theta^{(t)}} \big[ \ell_c(\theta) \mid Y = y \big]
	\label{eq:Qfunction}
\end{equation}


which corresponds to the Expectation step (E-step) of the EM algorithm. In the Maximization step (M-step), the parameter is updated by maximizing this function:

\begin{equation}
	\theta^{(t+1)} = \arg\max_{\theta} Q(\theta \mid \theta^{(t)}).
\end{equation}

The process is then iterated upon until $l(\theta^{t+1}) - l(\theta^t) <=  k$ for some arbitrary $k$ value.

\paragraph{Monotonicity}. A key property for the algorithm to work is for the likelihood to be monotonically increasing for each iteration. $L(\theta^{t+1})\geq L(\theta^{t}))$ which is discussed in Section 3.2 of \cite{mclachlan2008}.   





\subsection{Application of the EM algorithm to the deconvolution problem}


Let $X = (Z,Y)$ denote our complete data, that is in our deconvolution problem the observed age $Y$ and the corresponding true age $Z$. The PMF for $X$ is then:

\begin{equation}
	p_X(x = (z_i,y_j);\theta_Z) =  p_i M_{ij}
\end{equation}

where $M$ is the missclasification matrix defined in \ref{subsec:missclasification} and for a vector of $n$ independent observations  $\textbf{x} = {(z_1,y_1),(z_2,y_2),...,(z_n,y_n)}$ we obtain

\begin{equation}
	p_X(\textbf{x};\theta_Z) = \prod_i \prod_j (p_i M_{ij})^{n_{ij}}
\end{equation}

where $n_ij = \sum_{k=1}1\{X_k = i , Y_k =j\}$ is a random variable with the number of counts that were observed as $j$ given they were $i$.

The log-likelihood becomes: 

\begin{equation}
	\ell_c(\theta_Z) =  \sum_{i} \sum_{j} n_{ij} \log (p_i M_{ij}) = \sum_{i} \sum_{j} n_{ij} \log  M_{ij} + \sum_{i} \sum_{j} n_{ij} \log p_i 
\end{equation}

In accordance with equation \eqref{eq:Qfunction} we take the conditional expectation on $n_ij$ with an initial parameter $\theta^0$ conditioned on $Y$ 

\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}_{\theta^{(0)}} \big[ \ell_c(\theta) \mid Y = j \big] = 
\mathbf{E}_{\theta^{(0)}} \big[ \sum_{i} \sum_{j} n_{ij} \log (p_i M_{ij}) \mid Y = j \big] =
\sum_{i} \sum_{j} \mathbf{E}_{\theta^{(0)}} \big[n_{ij} \mid Y = j\big] \log (p_i M_{ij})
\end{equation}

The conditional expectation $E[n_ij | y =j]$ is the expected number of bears of age i missclasified as age j given the total amount of bears observed as age j.
 
Taking expectation:
\begin{equation}
	\mathbb{E}[n_{ij} \mid Y = y]
	=
	\sum_{k=1}^{n}
	\mathbb{E}\big[
	\mathbf{1}\{Z_k = i, Y_k = j\}
	\mid Y = y
	\big]
\end{equation}

Now conditioning on the observed $Y_k$:
\begin{equation}
	=
	\sum_{k : Y_k = j}
	P\big(
	Z_k = i \mid Y_k = j, \theta^{(0)}
	\big)
\end{equation}

Let $n_j = \#\{k : Y_k = j\}$ denote the number of observations with $Y=j$. Then:
\begin{equation}
	\mathbb{E}[n_{ij} \mid Y = y]
	=
	n_j \cdot
	P\big(
	Z = i \mid Y = j, \theta^{(0)}
	\big)
\end{equation}

Using Bayes' theorem:
\begin{equation}
	P\big(
	Z = i \mid Y = j, \theta^{(0)}
	\big)
	=
	\frac{
		p_i^{(0)} M_{ij}
	}{
		\sum_{i'} p_{i'}^{(0)} M_{i'j}
	}
\end{equation}

Thus:
\begin{equation}
	\mathbb{E}[n_{ij} \mid Y = y]
	=
	n_j \cdot
	\frac{
		p_i^{(0)} M_{ij}
	}{
		\sum_{i'} p_{i'}^{(0)} M_{i'j}
	}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Evaluation method}\label{sec:eva_method}


\paragraph{Total Varation (TV)}
To quantify the difference between two discrete probability distributions $Z$ and $Q$ over the same finite sample space $\mathcal{X}$, we use the \emph{Total Variation (TV) distance}:

\[
\text{TV}(P,Q) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} |P(x) - Q(x)|.
\]

The TV distance ranges from $0$ (distributions are identical) to $1$ (distributions have no overlap). 
Intuitively, it represents the maximum fraction of probability mass that must be reallocated to transform $P$ into $Q$. 
In this thesis, we use TV distance to assess how convolution and sampling affect the observed age distributions, as well as to evaluate the accuracy of deconvolution methods.

\section{Methods / Simulation Setup}

\subsection{Simulated Age Distributions}

A Leslie matrix $L$ of dimension $20 \times 20$ was used to model the age-structured population.
The first row represents the expected number of offspring produced by individuals in each age class, 
calculated as the product of three components for age class $i$:

\begin{equation}
	F_i = s_i \cdot p_{\text{coy},i} \cdot \text{litter}_i,
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $s_i$ is the survival probability from age $i$ to $i+1$,
	\item $p_{\text{coy},i}$ is the probability of conceiving at age $i$,
	\item $\text{litter}_i$ is the expected litter size at age $i$.
\end{itemize}

The survival and reproduction parameters were estimated from published graphs \cite{nina_bearmodel}. The full data vectors used to construct the Leslie matrix are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:leslie_params}.

\subsection{Error terms} \label{sec:error_terms}

To simulate plausible errors in age determination, we considered four different error models. Let $Z$ denote the true age, and $e$ the error term added to $Z$ in the convolution. We model the error as conditionally normal:

\[
e \mid Z \sim \mathcal{N}\big(\mu = f(Z), \sigma^2\big),
\]

and discretize the resulting values to the integer age bins (1--20) to match the observed age categories. 
Negative values were excluded, and the remaining probabilities were rescaled by $1 / (1 - F(e < 0))$. Values greater than 20 were assigned to the 19+ age class.

The following four specifications for $f(Z)$ (mean) and $\sigma$ (standard deviation) were considered:

\begin{enumerate} [label=\textbf{Model \Alph*:}, ref=\Alph*]
	\item \textbf{Model A Unbiased, low variance:} $f(Z) = 0$, \quad $\sigma = 1$ \label{model:A}
	\item \textbf{Model B Unbiased, high variance:} $f(Z) = 0$, \quad $\sigma = 4$ \label{model:B}
	\item \textbf{Model C Age-dependent bias, low variance:} $f(Z) = \dfrac{4}{Z + 0.5}$, \quad \label{model:C} $\sigma = 1$
	\item \textbf{Model D Age-dependent bias, high variance:} $f(Z) = \dfrac{4}{Z +0.5}$, \quad $\sigma = 4$ \label{model:D}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Convolution and Sampling Effects}

Having constructed the misclassification matrices for the four error models (Section~\ref{sec:error_terms}), we analytically convolve the true age distribution $p_Z(z)$ to obtain the distribution of observed ages $Y$:

\[
p_Y(y) = \sum_z M_{zy} \, p_Z(z),
\]

where $M$ is the misclassification matrix corresponding to each error model.

To assess the effects of finite sample sizes, we simulated random samples of size $n =  10, 25, 50,100$ from the convolved distribution $p_Y(y)$. This allows us to separate variability due to sampling error from information loss caused by the convolution.

The difference between the sampled distribution and the true age distribution was quantified using Total Variation distance.

\subsection{Deconvolution Methods}

Two deconvolution methods were used and compared: a ?naive? matrix inversion approach, and the EM algorithm described in Section~\ref{sec:em_alg}. For each error model introduced in Section~\ref{sec:error_terms}, the deconvolution was performed using both methods. The resulting estimates were evaluated using the KL-divergence, as described in Section~\ref{sec:kl_div}.

\paragraph{Matrix inversion}
The matrix inversion method directly inverts the misclassification matrix to estimate the underlying distribution, for negative estimates are set to 0 and the distribution is then normalized.. While straightforward, this approach can become unstable if the problem is ill-conditioned, potentially leading to negative or otherwise infeasible probabilities.

\paragraph{EM-algorithm deconvolution}
The EM algorithm iteratively estimates the underlying distribution by maximizing the expected complete-data log-likelihood (see Section~\ref{sec:em_alg}). This approach is more stable than direct inversion and accommodates the probabilistic nature of misclassification, providing estimates that remain valid probabilities.

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Matrix inversion:} 
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Identify when the problem is ill-posed (e.g., high condition number, negative probabilities).
	\end{itemize}
	\item \textbf{EM-algorithm:}
	\begin{itemize}
		\item Test stability with multiple starting points on the same convolved dataset.
		\item Evaluate how close the deconvolved distribution is to the true distribution.
		\item Explore the effect of different starting distributions (e.g., Poisson-distributed initial $p_i$).
	\end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\section{Matematiska formler och uttryck}

Den stora vinsten med att använda \LaTeX\ är att det blir lätt att skriva matematiska uttryck. Uttryck som skall stĺ inne i en text börjar med \$, sĺ här $P(X=k)=\binom{13}{k} (\frac{1}{3})^k (\frac{2}{3})^{13-k}$. Men kan ocksĺ lĺta uttrycket fĺ större utrymmme genom att använda \$\$:
$$
P(X=k)=\binom{13}{k} \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)^k \left( \frac{2}{3}\right)^{13-k}. % Här använder jag \left och \right för att matchande parenteser skall fĺ rätt storlek.
$$
eller \texttt{equation*}:
\begin{equation*}
P(X_3 \leq 4) = P(X_3 \geq 5) = \sum_{k=5}^{13}\binom{13}{k} \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)^k \left( \frac{2}{3}\right)^{13-k}
\end{equation*}
För flera ekvationer används lämpligen \texttt{align*}, där \texttt{\&} markerar vad ekvationerna ska ordnas efter:
\begin{align*}
P(Y \leq y) &= P\left(\frac{Y-\mu}{\sigma} \leq \frac{y-\mu}{\sigma} \right) \\
&= P\left(Z \leq \frac{y-\mu}{\sigma} \right) \\
&= \int_{-\infty}^{ (y-\mu)/\sigma } \frac{1}{ \sqrt{2\pi} } e^{ -\frac{z^2}{2} } dz \\
&= \int_{-\infty}^{ (y-\mu)/\sigma }\phi(z)dz \\
\{ \text{per def.} \}&= \Phi\left(\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma}\right)
\end{align*}
Vi använder \texttt{equation} och \texttt{align} med \texttt{*} eftersom vi inte vill ha nĺgon \textbf{ekvationsnumrering}. Man bör bara numrera ekvationer om man refererar till dem, och dĺ behöver de ocksĺ en \texttt{label}:
\begin{equation}\label{den_haer_aer_viktig}
\text{Bin}(n,p) \approx \text{Po}(np)\quad\text{dĺ $p$ är litet}
\end{equation}
% \quad ger lite tomt utrymme i formler. Du kan ocksĺ prova \! \, \: \; \qquad
Ett annat exempel:
\begin{equation}\label{ganska_basic}
1+1=2
\end{equation}
Pĺ kursen Sanno I lär man sig att uttryck \eqref{den_haer_aer_viktig} är sant, att ekvationen \eqref{ganska_basic} är sann vet du sedan tidigare. Poängen med att använda \texttt{label}s och \texttt{eqref} är att du slipper hĺlla reda pĺ de faktiska numrena. Prova att klippa ut koden till den andra ekvationen och lägga den före den första sĺ att det ser ut sĺ här: (kolla hur jag använder \texttt{verbatim} för längre kod-exempel!)

\begin{verbatim}
\begin{equation}\label{ganska_basic}
1+1=2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{den_haer_aer_viktig}
\text{Bin}(n,p) \approx \text{Po}(np)\quad\text{dĺ $p$ är litet}
\end{equation}
\end{verbatim}
Kompilera (kan behöva göras tvĺ gĺnger!), och kolla att resultatet stämmer.

\subsection{Felsökning}

Om \LaTeX\ spottar ur sig en massa varningar och felmeddelanden i nedre halvan av Emacs-fönstret beror det oftast pĺ att
\begin{enumerate}
\item du har skrivit en \{ eller en \} för mycket eller för litet, eller sĺ
\item har du skrivit ett eller tvĺ \$ för mycket eller för litet. 
\end{enumerate}
 Om du läser felmeddelandena sĺ brukar det stĺ vilken rad felet började pĺ (eller ĺtminstone var \LaTeX\ först upptäckte att nĺgot var fuffens). Använd \texttt{\%} pĺ olika ställen pĺ den raden eller i närheten för att kommentera bort kod som det kanske är nĺgot fel med, sĺ att du kan se var felet finns eller inte finns.


\section{Infoga bilder och plottar}\label{infoga_bilder}

Bilder är lätt att fĺ med. Om man använder först gör \texttt{dvi}-filer sĺ mĺste bilderna vara sparade som \texttt{eps}. Om man gör \texttt{pdf}-filer direkt sĺ mĺste bilderna vara sparade som just \texttt{pdf}. Du kan enkelt konvertera dina \texttt{eps}-filer till \texttt{pdf}-filer med kommandot \texttt{epstopdf}:
\begin{verbatim}
epstopdf plus_och_kryss.eps
\end{verbatim}

Det kan vara lite svĺrt att fĺ bilder att ligga exakt var man vill, eftersom \LaTeX\ har en egen uppfattning om vad som ser bäst ut. Kolla var figur \ref{en_figur} hamnade, och hur jag refererade till den med \texttt{ref}




\section{Att referera, citera, och mer att läsa}

Man kan referera till vad som helst du har gett en \texttt{label} med kommandot \texttt{ref}. \texttt{eqref} lägger dessutom till parenteser kring (ekvations-)numret. Man kan t.ex. hänvisa till stycke \ref{om_text} som handlar om Emacs. Om din \texttt{pdf}- eller \texttt{dvi}-läsare klarar av hyperlänkar sĺ är dessutom dina referser klickbara eftersom vi använder packaget \texttt{hyperref}.\footnote{Prova att ta bort \texttt{colorlinks=true} pĺ rad 6 innan du skall skriva ut.}

Om du vill citera en källa gör du det enklast med \texttt{cite} sĺ här:  Jag har lärt mig mycket om \LaTeX\ frĺn \cite{lshort}. Om man vill veta mer om detaljer för att typsätta matematik bör man även titta pĺ \cite{amsmath}.
